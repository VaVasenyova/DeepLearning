Gender (Sex) - The Most Critical Factor

Gender was the strongest predictor of survival on the Titanic. The "women and children first" protocol was strictly followed during the evacuation.

Evidence: Women had a 74% survival rate compared to only 19% for men - a dramatic 55 percentage point difference.

This factor alone explains more variance in survival outcomes than any other variable in the dataset, including passenger class or age.

Passenger Class (Pclass) - Strong Socioeconomic Indicator

Passenger class strongly correlated with survival chances, reflecting both cabin location and social priorities during evacuation.

Evidence: First-class passengers had a 63% survival rate, while third-class passengers had only a 24% survival rate.

This 39 percentage point gap demonstrates how socioeconomic status directly impacted survival probability.

Age - Children Had Priority

While not as dominant as gender, age was still a significant factor, with children given priority during evacuation.

Evidence: Children under 10 had approximately a 60% survival rate compared to about 38% for adults.

When combined with gender (young females), survival rates exceeded 80%, showing the compounding effect of these factors.

Conclusion

Based on comprehensive analysis of the Titanic dataset, gender (Sex) was the single most important factor determining survival. The cultural imperative to save women first created the largest disparity in survival rates across any variable.

Passenger class (Pclass) was the second most important factor, revealing how socioeconomic status influenced access to lifeboats.

These findings align with historical accounts of the disaster and demonstrate how social norms and shipboard protocols directly impacted survival outcomes.
